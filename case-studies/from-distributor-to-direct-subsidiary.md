# Case Study

# From Distributor to Direct Subsidiary

> *Changing the route to market is rarely a sales decision. It is one of the most strategic decisions an international company can make.*

---

# Executive Perspective

Many international companies eventually reach a strategic crossroads.

The distributor model that successfully introduced the brand into a market no longer provides the capabilities required for the next stage of growth.

At this point, executives face an important question:

**Should we continue developing the distributor relationship, or is the time right to establish a direct subsidiary?**

There is no universal answer.

The decision depends on commercial maturity, market potential, organisational readiness and long-term strategic objectives.

---

# Why Companies Consider the Change

Distributor models offer clear advantages during the early stages of market development.

They provide:

- lower investment;
- local market knowledge;
- entrepreneurial execution;
- speed to market.

As markets mature, however, new priorities often emerge.

Companies increasingly seek:

- closer customer relationships;
- greater control over commercial execution;
- stronger brand positioning;
- integrated education and marketing;
- improved data visibility;
- direct strategic alignment.

These ambitions frequently trigger the discussion about establishing a direct organisation.

---

# Experience

Throughout my career I have worked with both distributor-led and direct market structures across Europe.

This included evaluating commercial models, supporting distributor development, strengthening existing partnerships and participating in the preparation of direct subsidiaries.

One consistent observation emerged.

The success of a transition depends far less on legal structures than on organisational readiness.

A company can establish a subsidiary overnight.

Building a high-performing commercial organisation takes years.

---

# Questions Every Executive Team Should Ask

Before changing the route to market, leadership should evaluate several strategic questions.

## Market

Is the market sufficiently developed to justify direct investment?

---

## Organisation

Does the company possess the leadership capability required to manage a direct operation?

---

## Customers

Will customers genuinely receive greater value through a direct organisation?

---

## Economics

Will the long-term financial benefits justify the increased complexity and investment?

---

## Timing

Is this the right moment, or would further development of the distributor relationship create greater value?

---

# Common Mistakes

Transitions often underperform because organisations:

- underestimate implementation complexity;
- focus on structure instead of capability;
- communicate insufficiently with customers;
- neglect organisational culture;
- recruit too late;
- underestimate operational readiness;
- fail to preserve the strengths of the previous distributor model.

The objective should never be to replace a distributor.

The objective should be to build a stronger commercial organisation.

---

# Leadership Lessons

Several principles have consistently proven valuable.

## Respect the Existing Partnership

Distributors often played a crucial role in building the market.

Transitions should recognise that contribution.

---

## Build Before You Launch

Customers should experience continuity rather than disruption.

Preparation determines success.

---

## Invest in Leadership

The first leadership team defines the culture of the new organisation.

Choosing the right people matters more than organisational charts.

---

## Think Beyond Day One

A successful launch is only the beginning.

The real challenge is building an organisation capable of outperforming year after year.

# Closing Reflection

Whether an organisation chooses a distributor, a direct subsidiary or a hybrid model is ultimately less important than the quality of leadership behind that decision.

The most successful international companies are not defined by their organisational structure.

They are defined by their ability to build commercial organisations that consistently create value for customers while remaining adaptable as markets evolve.
