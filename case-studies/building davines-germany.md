# Case Study

# Building Davines Germany: From Distributor to Direct Subsidiary

> *Building a subsidiary is not simply a legal exercise. It is the process of creating an organisation capable of delivering sustainable long-term growth while preserving customer trust during fundamental organisational change.*

---

# Executive Summary

One of the most significant projects during my career at Davines Group was supporting the transition of the German business from an independent distributor model to a wholly owned German subsidiary.

The objective extended far beyond changing the legal structure.

The ambition was to establish a scalable commercial organisation that would strengthen customer relationships, improve commercial execution and support the company's long-term growth strategy in one of Europe's largest professional beauty markets.

---

# Business Context

Germany represented one of Davines' most important strategic markets.

The existing distributor had contributed significantly to the brand's development over many years.

At the same time, the company's long-term international strategy increasingly favoured direct market organisations capable of creating closer customer relationships, stronger commercial alignment and greater organisational control.

The transition therefore required balancing two priorities:

- protecting an established business;
- building a new organisation for future growth.

---

# Executive Challenge

Creating a direct subsidiary affects almost every aspect of a business.

The project required coordinated decisions across commercial, operational and organisational dimensions.

Key considerations included:

- organisational design;
- commercial leadership;
- customer communication;
- logistics;
- supply chain;
- warehousing;
- information systems;
- finance;
- human resources;
- legal transition;
- distributor exit;
- commercial continuity.

Each workstream depended upon the successful coordination of the others.

---

# My Role

Within this transformation I contributed to the commercial development and operational preparation of the future German organisation.

My responsibilities included supporting:

- commercial organisation design;
- sales strategy;
- commercial planning;
- forecasting;
- distributor transition;
- customer continuity;
- trade marketing;
- commercial processes;
- recruitment of key commercial roles;
- collaboration across international functions.

The objective was not simply to launch a subsidiary.

It was to establish an organisation capable of performing from its first day of operation.

---

# Leadership Lessons

Several important leadership principles emerged throughout the project.

## Transformation requires trust.

Customers, employees and business partners support change when communication is transparent and leadership remains consistent.

---

## Preparation determines execution.

The visible launch represents only a small proportion of the overall work.

Successful execution depends upon months of preparation across numerous interconnected functions.

---

## Commercial continuity is essential.

Customers should experience organisational improvement rather than operational disruption.

Protecting customer confidence remains one of the highest priorities during organisational transformation.

---

## Cross-functional collaboration creates success.

Commercial, operations, finance, IT, logistics, marketing and HR all contributed to the successful transition.

No single department could have achieved the objective independently.

---

# Broader Perspective

Although every subsidiary creation is unique, several principles apply consistently.

Successful organisations:

- prepare earlier than expected;
- invest heavily in leadership;
- communicate transparently;
- develop local capabilities;
- maintain customer focus throughout the transition.

Building a subsidiary is therefore not simply a market entry exercise.

It is an organisational transformation programme.

# Closing Reflection

Looking back, the creation of Davines Germany reinforced one belief that has guided much of my career.

Commercial success is ultimately created by organisations.

Products matter.

Brands matter.

Strategy matters.

But sustainable growth depends on building organisations that are capable of translating strategic ambition into consistent customer value over many years.
